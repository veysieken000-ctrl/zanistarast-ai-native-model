## 9. Cross-Domain Convergence

One of the central observations of Zanistarast is that its structural principles appear across multiple domains:

- natural systems  
- computational systems  
- human-designed structures  
- philosophical and religious descriptions  

This does not imply that Zanistarast imposes these structures,  
but rather that it attempts to model patterns that are already present.

---

### 9.1 Natural Systems

In nature:

- physical laws maintain consistency (validation)
- energy and matter flow (circulation)
- biological systems perform defined roles (function)
- systems stabilize or collapse (outcomes)

---

### 9.2 Computational Systems

In computing:

- all processes originate from defined states (unity)
- systems rely on validation (authentication, logic checks)
- resources are distributed and processed (circulation)
- programs execute defined functions (alignment)
- systems either succeed or fail (terminal states)

---

### 9.3 Human Systems

In human-designed systems:

- governance depends on rule consistency
- economies depend on resource flow
- organizations depend on role alignment

Failure in any of these leads to instability.

---

### 9.4 Philosophical and Religious Descriptions

Many philosophical and religious traditions describe:

- unity
- order
- justice
- accountability

Zanistarast does not assume these as proofs,  
but examines whether such descriptions correspond to observable system structures.

---

## 10. Core Claim

Zanistarast does not claim to introduce entirely new principles.

Instead, it proposes that:

> The same structural logic is already present across nature, technology, and human systems.

The framework attempts to:

- identify  
- formalize  
- and test  

these recurring patterns.

---

## 11. Hypothesis

If a system:

- maintains coherence (validation)
- sustains circulation (resource flow)
- preserves alignment (function)

then:

→ it tends toward stability.

If it fails in these:

→ it tends toward collapse.

---

## 12. Conclusion

Zanistarast is not merely a theoretical construction.

It is an attempt to describe a structure that may already be:

- embedded in natural processes  
- implemented in computational systems  
- reflected in human organization  

The task of Zanistarast is to make this structure:

- explicit  
- testable  
- and comparable across domains
